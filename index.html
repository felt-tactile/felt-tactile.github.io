Abstract

The sense of touch is central to manipulation, especially when vision is occluded or ambiguous. Although combining vision and touch improves manipulation, learning robust visuo-tactile policies requires substantial tactile data. Such data remains scarcer than visual data, because tactile sensors are fragile, specialized, and hard to standardize.

To address this, we present Feature-Extracted Latent Tactile (FELT), a learning-based framework that synthesizes per-finger pressure tactile images from RGB observations, reducing the need for tactile-equipped data collection. FELT uses a large frozen visual encoder and a lightweight query decoder to predict tactile signals in a single feed-forward pass. To respect the physical topology of dual-finger tactile sensors, FELT decodes the left and right tactile sensor panels through separate branches, capturing the asymmetric contact patterns during interactions such as wiping, insertion, and in-hand rotation. Unlike prior tactile augmentation methods, which rely on tactile references, FELT requires only RGB data at inference time. This lets it retroactively augment large vision-only datasets with tactile channels for training visuo-tactile policies, while also providing tactile signals at runtime to robots that lack physical tactile sensors. Experiments on four contact-rich manipulation tasks demonstrate that FELT produces physically grounded tactile signals and improves downstream visuo-tactile policy success over vision-only baselines.
